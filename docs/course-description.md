# AP Pre-Calculus Course Description

## Overview

An interactive intelligent textbook for AP Pre-Calculus designed to prepare
students for AP Pre-Calculus tests. This course follows the College Board
AP Pre-Calculus curriculum framework, which builds a deep understanding
of functions and their properties as essential preparation for calculus
and other college-level mathematics courses.

AP Pre-Calculus was introduced by the College Board in the 2023–2024
school year to create a nationally standardized pre-calculus course
that emphasizes function modeling, reasoning with functions, and
making connections across multiple representations.

This course features a large collection of Micro-Simulations (MicroSims)
that are used by the students to visualize complex topics.

## Target Audience

- **Primary Audience:** High school students (typically grades 10–12) preparing for the AP Pre-Calculus exam and future AP Calculus coursework
- **Prerequisites:** Successful completion of Algebra 2 or equivalent, including familiarity with linear and quadratic functions, basic polynomial operations, systems of equations, and foundational coordinate geometry
- **Reading Level:** Senior High School (grades 10–12)

## Course Structure

The course is organized into four units following the College Board AP Pre-Calculus framework. Units 1–3 are tested on the AP exam. Unit 4 is enrichment content not included on the AP exam but valuable for calculus readiness.

| Unit | Topic | Key Concepts |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Polynomial and Rational Functions | Rates of change, polynomial behavior, rational functions, end behavior, zeros, asymptotes, transformations |
| 2 | Exponential and Logarithmic Functions | Exponential growth/decay, logarithmic functions, compositions, inverses, data modeling, semi-log plots |
| 3 | Trigonometric and Polar Functions | Periodic phenomena, unit circle, sine/cosine/tangent, inverse trig, polar coordinates, polar function graphs |
| 4 | Functions Involving Parameters, Vectors, and Matrices (Not on AP Exam) | Parametric functions, vectors, matrices, linear transformations |

## Topics Covered

### Unit 1: Polynomial and Rational Functions

1. **Change in Tandem** — how two quantities change together
2. **Rates of Change** — average rates of change and their meaning
3. **Rates of Change in Linear and Quadratic Functions** — constant vs. changing rates
4. **Polynomial Functions and Rates of Change** — higher-degree polynomial behavior
5. **Polynomial Functions and Complex Zeros** — zeros over real and complex numbers
6. **Polynomial Functions and End Behavior** — leading term dominance
7. **Rational Functions and End Behavior** — horizontal and slant asymptotes
8. **Rational Functions and Zeros** — numerator zeros, holes, and vertical asymptotes
9. **Rational Functions and Vertical Asymptotes** — unbounded behavior near excluded values
10. **Transformations of Functions** — translations, reflections, dilations
11. **Function Model Selection and Assumption Articulation** — choosing appropriate models
12. **Function Model Construction and Application** — building models from data

### Unit 2: Exponential and Logarithmic Functions

1. **Change in Arithmetic and Geometric Sequences** — additive vs. multiplicative patterns
2. **Change in Linear and Exponential Functions** — constant rate vs. proportional rate
3. **Exponential Functions** — base, growth factor, and decay factor
4. **Exponential Function Manipulation** — equivalent forms and properties
5. **Exponential Function Context and Data Modeling** — real-world applications
6. **Competing Function Model Validation** — comparing model fits
7. **Composition of Functions** — combining functions
8. **Inverse Functions** — reversing input-output relationships
9. **Logarithmic Expressions** — definition and properties of logarithms
10. **Logarithmic Functions** — graphs, domains, ranges
11. **Logarithmic Function Context and Data Modeling** — linearizing exponential data
12. **Semi-Log Plots** — interpreting data on logarithmic scales

### Unit 3: Trigonometric and Polar Functions

1. **Periodic Phenomena** — recognizing repeating patterns
2. **Sine, Cosine, and Tangent** — definitions from the unit circle
3. **Sine and Cosine Function Values** — special angles and reference angles
4. **Sine and Cosine Function Graphs** — amplitude, period, midline
5. **Sinusoidal Functions** — modeling with sine and cosine
6. **Sinusoidal Function Context and Data Modeling** — real-world periodic data
7. **Sinusoidal Function Transformations** — phase shift, vertical shift, amplitude, period
8. **The Tangent Function** — definition, graph, and properties
9. **Inverse Trigonometric Functions** — arcsine, arccosine, arctangent
10. **Trigonometric Equations and Inequalities** — solving for angles
11. **The Secant, Cosecant, and Cotangent Functions** — reciprocal trig functions
12. **Equivalent Representations of Trigonometric Functions** — identities and co-functions
13. **Trigonometry and Polar Coordinates** — converting between coordinate systems
14. **Polar Function Graphs** — roses, limaçons, circles, cardioids
15. **Rates of Change in Polar Functions** — how polar curves change

### Unit 4: Functions Involving Parameters, Vectors, and Matrices (Not on AP Exam)

1. **Parametric Functions** — expressing x and y as functions of a parameter
2. **Parametric Functions Modeling Planar Motion** — position, velocity, direction
3. **Parametric Functions and Rates of Change** — derivatives of parametric curves
4. **Vectors** — magnitude, direction, component form
5. **Vector-Valued Functions** — functions that return vectors
6. **Matrices** — operations, dimensions, properties
7. **Matrices as Functions and Linear Transformations** — geometric transformations using matrices
8. **Matrices Modeling Contexts** — applications of matrix operations

## Topics NOT Covered

To maintain focus on pre-calculus foundations, this course does **not** cover:

- **Limits** — formal definition and evaluation of limits (covered in AP Calculus)
- **Derivatives** — instantaneous rates of change and differentiation rules (covered in AP Calculus)
- **Integrals** — antiderivatives and area under curves (covered in AP Calculus)
- **Formal proofs** — rigorous mathematical proofs (covered in discrete mathematics)
- **Statistics and probability** — data analysis and inference (covered in AP Statistics)
- **Conic sections in depth** — parabolas, ellipses, hyperbolas beyond what appears in function contexts
- **Series and sequences beyond arithmetic/geometric** — convergence, Taylor series (covered in AP Calculus BC)
- **Linear algebra beyond Unit 4 basics** — eigenvalues, vector spaces (covered in college linear algebra)
- **Differential equations** — solving equations involving derivatives (covered in post-calculus courses)

## Learning Objectives (2001 Bloom's Taxonomy)

### Level 1: Remember

1. Recall the definitions of polynomial, rational, exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric functions
2. State the properties of exponents and logarithms
3. List the special angle values for sine, cosine, and tangent on the unit circle
4. Identify the standard forms of polynomial, exponential, and sinusoidal functions
5. Define key vocabulary: asymptote, zero, end behavior, period, amplitude, midline, domain, range

### Level 2: Understand

6. Explain the relationship between average rate of change and the behavior of a function over an interval
7. Describe how the degree and leading coefficient of a polynomial determine its end behavior
8. Interpret the meaning of zeros, vertical asymptotes, and holes in rational functions
9. Explain the relationship between exponential and logarithmic functions as inverses
10. Describe how the parameters of a sinusoidal function (amplitude, period, phase shift, midline) affect its graph
11. Explain the connection between the unit circle and the graphs of trigonometric functions
12. Interpret polar coordinates and describe how polar graphs differ from Cartesian graphs
13. Explain the distinction between arithmetic and geometric sequences in terms of additive vs. multiplicative change

### Level 3: Apply

14. Calculate average rates of change for polynomial, rational, exponential, and trigonometric functions over specified intervals
15. Graph polynomial functions using zeros, multiplicity, and end behavior
16. Graph rational functions using zeros, vertical asymptotes, holes, and horizontal/slant asymptotes
17. Apply transformation rules (translations, reflections, dilations) to function graphs
18. Convert between exponential and logarithmic forms of equations
19. Use composition of functions to build new functions from existing ones
20. Find inverse functions algebraically and verify the inverse relationship
21. Evaluate trigonometric functions using the unit circle and reference angles
22. Solve trigonometric equations for specific angle values within given domains
23. Convert between rectangular and polar coordinates
24. Graph polar functions including roses, limaçons, and cardioids
25. Use parametric equations to model planar motion
26. Perform matrix operations including addition, multiplication, and scalar multiplication

### Level 4: Analyze

27. Analyze the rate of change of a function to determine whether it is increasing, decreasing, concave up, or concave down over an interval
28. Compare and contrast polynomial, rational, exponential, and trigonometric function models for a given data set
29. Determine which function type best models a given real-world scenario based on the pattern of change
30. Analyze the relationship between a function and its inverse, including domain and range restrictions
31. Distinguish between situations modeled by linear vs. exponential growth and justify the choice
32. Analyze how changes in parameters of a sinusoidal function correspond to changes in the real-world phenomenon being modeled
33. Examine semi-log plots to determine whether data exhibits exponential behavior
34. Analyze the behavior of polar functions by examining how the radius changes as the angle varies

### Level 5: Evaluate

35. Assess the validity of a proposed function model by comparing predicted vs. actual values
36. Evaluate competing function models (polynomial, exponential, logarithmic, sinusoidal) and justify which provides the best fit for a data set
37. Judge whether a function model remains appropriate outside its original domain of data collection
38. Critique claims about function behavior based on evidence from graphs, tables, and algebraic representations
39. Evaluate the reasonableness of solutions to trigonometric equations within real-world contexts
40. Assess the strengths and limitations of different coordinate systems (rectangular vs. polar) for representing specific curves

### Level 6: Create

41. Construct a piecewise-defined function to model a real-world situation with different behaviors over different intervals
42. Build a sinusoidal model from real-world periodic data (e.g., tides, temperatures, daylight hours)
43. Develop an exponential or logarithmic model from raw data, including determining appropriate parameters
44. Create a rational function model with specified zeros, asymptotes, and intercepts to match given constraints
45. Design a polar equation to produce a curve with specified symmetry and shape properties
46. Formulate a parametric model to represent the motion of an object in the plane given initial conditions

## AP Exam Format

The AP Pre-Calculus exam consists of:

- **Section I: Multiple Choice (62.5% of score)**
    - Part A: 28 questions, 80 minutes (no calculator)
    - Part B: 12 questions, 40 minutes (graphing calculator required)
- **Section II: Free Response (37.5% of score)**
    - Part A: 2 questions, 30 minutes (graphing calculator required)
    - Part B: 2 questions, 30 minutes (no calculator)

Only content from **Units 1–3** appears on the AP exam. Unit 4 is designated as non-AP exam content.

## Assessment Within This Textbook

Learning is assessed through:

- **Chapter Quizzes** — Multiple-choice questions aligned to specific learning objectives at the end of each chapter
- **Interactive MicroSims** — Hands-on explorations that build conceptual understanding through dynamic visualizations
- **Practice Problems** — Exercises progressing from basic skills to AP-level application and analysis
